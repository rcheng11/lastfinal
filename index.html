Gang Violence and the State of Emergency in El Salvador

By: Ron Cheng

The current regime in El Salvador has been struggling with the widespread influence of pandilleros (gangsters), the rise of which ​​award-winning Salvadoran investigative journalist Oscar Martinez credits to the violence produced by El Salvador's complicated 12 year civil war , US involvement, and the lack of a proper conclusion in his interview for the New American Story project.

Friends and relatives mourn Ricardo Amador, a bus driver killed by gang members, in Aguilares, El Salvador, in September 2016.

Consequently, the rise of several pandillas like Barrio 18 Sureños (B18-S), Barrio 18 Revolucionarios (B18-R), and Mara Salvatrucha 13 (MS-13) have uprooted lives at every stage of living, and disrupt government reforms and efforts. Everyday civilians face extortion, kidnappings, assault, and execution at the hands of pandilleros.

The formation of gang zones have had tremendous political, social, and economic impact because gangs have grown into political, social, and economic forces that often challenge government authority

Youth in gang zones are drawn to join gangs to obtain a cultural identity and belonging, rather than for financial gain.

The average gang member becomes satisfied with obtaining status and identity, allowing the majority of extortion money (Martinez estimates that MS-13 alone accrues 31 million a year, ) flow to the top.

Hence, joining a pandilla not only keeps those who join in poverty, but also impoverishes its victims.

Meanwhile, major gangs compete for territory and sign peace agreements between each other.

Consequently, nearly every municipality in El Salvador is occupied by some form of pandilla presence, and according to Martinez around 60% of the country is run predominately by pandillas like

The situation is so dire that rates of violent crime in some years surpass those in countries at war.

So why not fight this like a war?

President Nayib Bukele

That is what El Salvador’s current President Nayib Bukele, who jokes that he is “coolest dictator in the world”, is trying to do.

By establishing a state of emergency (regimen de excepcion) since March 2022, Bukele has begun a crackdown that suspends the constitutional rights of the citizens of El Salvador. While this return to the “mano dura” (iron fist) style policies satisfies public demands for gang crackdowns, it has also led to human rights violations and corruption.

But Bukele’s policies are exacerbating El Salvador’s cycle of perpetual violence .

Human rights organizations like WOLA and Amnesty International call into attention the ways that the power granted to Bukele and his administration under the state of emergency has led to abuse and corruption.

Relatives of those detained during the state of emergency protesting

Arrests are often indiscriminate with loose criteria (taken just for being nervous) and include former gang members (just having tattoos is evidence). Many are arrested through mass hearings, where individual cases are not heard.

Bukele has also used his power under the state of emergency to silence some of his critics, arresting them under crimes of contempt.

Inmates at Izalco prison in San Salvador rounded up during a security operation on April 25, 2020.

With nearly 50,000 alleged gang members imprisoned since the start of the state of emergency, El Salvador now has the highest incarceration rate in the world. Local organizations sound the alarm on these overcrowded detention centers, where prisoners lack basic human necessities.

Alleged members of the Barrio 18 gang are presented to the media after being arrested on charges of the disappearance or murder of several people.

Using powers under the state of emergency, Bukele has put out a mandate that prevents citizens from viewing how the government spends public funds. Additionally, Bukele has limited access to other previously public information and reduced verification checks leading to discrepancies in statistics like deaths and disappearances.

So will Bukele’s brutal crackdown under the state of emergency work?

Past policies have already tried both a mano dura approach and a reformist approach. However, alone they both have failed for their own reasons: crackdowns don’t address root problems and reforms take too long to turnover to satisfy public demands and are difficult to execute in regions dominated by pandillas.

The current abuse of the state of emergency, clearly, is not the answer to El Salvador’s gang problem. Approaches vary, but should seek to rein in gang violence without resorting to indiscriminate state violence. The influence of pandillas in El Salvador is no joke: there are more pandilleros than police and at least 8% of the population are calmados, a term used for their sympathizers and former members. That’s why some argue that negotiations can’t be avoided. Instead, some argue that negotiations (between governments and pandillas) can be used to rein in the violence while the government takes steps to destabilize the pandillas and bring lasting reform that can end El Salvador’s cycle of violence.

So what can you do?

  • Maintain political pressure on Bukele and the Salvadoran Legislative Assembly so that the State of Emergency is not extended further.
  • Maintain political pressure on the United States to avoid massive deportations (that El Salvador cannot currently handle), and redesignate El Salvador for Temporary Protected Status (TPS).
